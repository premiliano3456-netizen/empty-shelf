The Empty Shelf: How the Union Naval Blockade Shaped Confederate Civilian Life in 1862

An Interactive History of Scarcity

Research Question

How did the tightening Union naval blockade in 1862 translate from military policy into everyday scarcity for ordinary Confederate civilians?

Thesis

By 1862, the Union naval blockade had moved beyond a military abstraction to reshape the daily material lives of Confederate civilians through a cumulative, uneven process felt differently by different goods and communities as the year progressed.

The Blockade Tightens

In April 1861, President Lincoln declared a naval blockade of Confederate ports. In 1861, approximately 90 percent of Confederate ships still broke through. By 1862, that figure had dropped to less than 15 percent. What began as a military policy became a daily reality for ordinary Confederate civilians, as goods they depended on began to disappear from shelves, homes, and tables one by one.1

Coffee · Spring 1862

Coffee Disappears — Spring 1862

Why it disappeared

Coffee was almost entirely imported. The blockade cut off Southern ports from foreign trade almost immediately after its declaration. By early 1862, coffee shipments had stopped reaching most Confederate towns. Civilians turned to substitutes including chicory, rye, and roasted sweet potato.

What it cost

Before the war, coffee cost approximately 15 cents per pound. By mid-1862 it was nearly impossible to find at any price in many Confederate towns. The Confederate dollar, which stood at parity with the US dollar in 1861, had already begun losing value, meaning civilians were paying more for less with currency worth less every month.

What the blockade did

The American Pageant notes that "customs duties were choked off as the coils of the Union blockade tightened," cutting off not only imported goods but the revenue the Confederate government needed to pay for the war itself.2

Sugar · April 1862

Sugar Becomes a Luxury — April 1862

Why it disappeared

Louisiana was the Confederacy's primary sugar-producing state and home of its largest port, New Orleans. On April 25, 1862, Union naval forces under Captain David Farragut captured New Orleans, severing one of the Confederacy's most important supply and production hubs in a single blow. With New Orleans gone, both the domestic supply and the import route for sugar collapsed simultaneously.

What it cost

Sugar prices rose sharply after the fall of New Orleans as supply dried up faster than demand. For Confederate civilians, sugar shifted from a common household staple to an occasional luxury within weeks of the city's capture.

What this means

The fall of New Orleans in April 1862 is the single most important event explaining why 1862 was the turning point year for Confederate civilian scarcity. It was not a slow tightening. It was a sudden, specific loss that civilians felt immediately.3

Salt · Mid-1862

Salt Runs Out — Mid-1862

Why it disappeared

Salt was not a luxury. It was the primary means of preserving meat before refrigeration. Without salt, meat rotted. The Confederacy had very few domestic salt works and relied heavily on imports that the blockade cut off. The shortage of salt therefore threatened not just salt itself but the entire meat supply.

What it cost

Salt that cost approximately 65 cents per sack before the war rose to as much as $60 per sack in some Confederate states by late 1862. This was not inflation in the ordinary sense. This was a good becoming functionally unavailable to ordinary civilians regardless of what they were willing to pay.

What this means

Salt is the clearest example of how the blockade's effects compounded. It did not simply make one item scarce. It made every item that depended on salt scarce as well.4

Flour & Bread · Spring–Summer 1862

Empty Bread Boxes — Spring Through Summer 1862

Why it disappeared

Flour supplies dwindled as grain imports stopped and Confederate transportation networks were disrupted. Civilian steamboats were pressed into military service, cutting off the supply routes that towns and households depended on.

Civilian voice

Mary Guthrie Latta was a soldier's wife in Dyersburg, Tennessee, managing a household alone with young children while her husband served as a captain in the 13th Tennessee Infantry, CSA. She was not a public figure. She had no audience. She was writing privately to her husband on April 6, 1862, about her daily life. When she mentions wanting her cousin to visit but preferring to wait "until we get something to eat," she is not making a political statement. She is stating a fact of daily life in a single offhand clause, which tells us more about how ordinary Confederate civilians experienced scarcity than any official report could. She also notes that the steamboat Ben McCullough "has been pressed into the service" and can no longer make civilian runs, documenting the disruption of civilian supply routes in specific, verifiable terms.

"I would like very much to see her, still am not very sorry that her visit is to be postponed for awhile until we get something to eat."
— Mary Guthrie Latta, Dyersburg, Tennessee, April 6, 18625
Medicine · Throughout 1862

Medicine Runs Dry — Throughout 1862

Why it disappeared

The Confederacy depended almost entirely on imported medicines including quinine and other imported medical supplies. The blockade cut off these imports almost immediately. Confederate surgeons were forced to experiment with herbal substitutes, and civilians had even less access than the army, which at least had official supply channels however depleted.

What it cost

Basic medicines became essentially unavailable to civilians in many areas by mid-1862 regardless of price. Illness that was treatable before the war became life-threatening not because medicine had become expensive but because it had ceased to exist on the civilian market.

What this means

The disappearance of medicine from civilian life is the starkest example of how the blockade affected Confederate civilians differently from Confederate soldiers. The army had a supply system, however failing. Civilians had only the open market, and the open market had nothing to sell.6

Cotton Cloth · Mid–Late 1862

Homespun Takes Over — Mid to Late 1862

Why it disappeared

The Confederacy exported raw cotton but could no longer import the manufactured cloth made from it. Textile factories were overwhelmingly located in the North and in Britain. Confederate women returned to spinning and weaving at home, producing rough homespun fabric as a substitute for the manufactured cloth that had disappeared from stores.

What this means

The disappearance of cotton cloth from Confederate shelves is one of the war's deepest ironies. The Confederacy was built on cotton. It wagered its independence on European demand for cotton. And yet its own civilians could not obtain cloth made from it because the manufacturing capacity was on the other side of the blockade.

Cultural response

Dr. Ross-Nazzal's lecture notes reference Confederate women taking pride in "denying themselves the silks and satins of their Northern sisters" and a popular song celebrating "the homespun dress the Southern ladies wear." This cultural response to scarcity is itself evidence of how deeply the blockade had reshaped daily life by mid-1862.7

End of Year Shelf

What Remains

By the end of 1862, the shelves of a Confederate general store looked very different from January. The blockade had not simply raised prices. It had removed goods from civilian life entirely. Confederate soldiers writing home documented the same deterioration from the field. Jacob B. Click, a young cavalryman from Rockingham County, Virginia, was writing love letters to a girl back home, not a military report. The fact that food scarcity intrudes so insistently into correspondence that is otherwise entirely about romance and social life is itself significant. It suggests that scarcity was not an abstract concern but an inescapable daily fact by the fall of 1862.

"Som times we wold not git any thing to eat in 4 days but som corn that we wold steal out of the corn fields."
— Jacob B. Click, Virginia, September 18, 18628

Closing Analysis

What 1862 Set in Motion

The blockade's effects in 1862 were not yet catastrophic for every Confederate civilian in every location. They were cumulative and accelerating, and they were felt unevenly, hitting imported goods first, then domestic goods that depended on imported inputs like salt and medicine, then the transportation networks that moved everything else. The Confederate dollar stood at parity with the US dollar in 1861. By 1863 it had fallen to 63 cents. By 1865 it was worth less than 2 cents. In 1863, Confederate women in Richmond looted stores for food the market could no longer supply. One breakfast for three in Richmond in 1864 cost $141. The Confederacy ultimately experienced an inflation rate of approximately 9,000 percent over the course of the war. All of that began in 1862, item by item, on shelves like this one.9
